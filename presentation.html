Hi, my name is Kathit, as you may already know. My essential question is, "Why should we discriminate against anti-vaxxers?"

I needed to know how media reacts to anti-vaxxers because it portrays things to the public that makes people believe it.

A CBC news report stated that anti-vaxxers like to scare others to not vaccinate their children by using websites like Vaccine Choice Canada. Vaccine Choice Canada posts stories of parents who took their healthy children for their scheduled vaccines and left the hospital with "vaccine-injured" children. Hence, the CBC reporter wrote that people should hijack their "scaring parents" method. She stated that by showing anti-vaxxers pictures of children hospitalized with measles-induced encephalitis, anti-vaxxers would deny vaccination.

This method could work for some anti-vaxxers, but not for other anti-vaxxers who can counter with facts like encephalitis from measles is very rare and vaccines do not prevent us from all kinds of meningitis

NBC News showed how anti-vaccination is spreading on social media by sharing a story of a woman who lost her child because of a group named "Stop Mandatory Vaccination.”

The "Stop Mandatory Vaccination" group is a Facebook group created by a guy named Larry Cook. Lots ask him for advice, but they need to understand that he does not have any medical expertise or any children. He is a just stranger like most of the people in that Facebook group.

Facebook reacted and said it would limit anti-vaccine reach by no longer serving up anti-vaccine groups and pages in search results and the recommendations bar. They will no longer allow users and groups to spread vaccine misinformation, such as place ads or run fundraisers. However, Facebook still has not deleted the "Stop Mandatory Vaccination" group. YouTube and Pinterest have also limited the reach of anti-vaccination. Amazon has started removing books about anti-vaccination.

If anti-vaccination is portrayed so severely in media and in general, then why do people decide not to vaccinate their child.

The answer is mainly religious and safety reasons.

The animal-derived gelatin used in producing some vaccines and the human fetus tissue used in the rubella component poses the most significant concern for religions. Safety reasons are the side effects of vaccination. Minor side effects include fever and mild rash, moderate side effects include pain and stiffness of the joints, seizure, and low platelet count, and severe side effects include allergic reactions, which can cause hives, swelling, and trouble breathing. However, it is infrequent for that to happen.

Religious reasons are why most parents are hesitant or, in simple terms, confused about vaccination

Parents are always hesitant. When children ask parents to let them go somewhere like a party, parents are afraid that something might happen to them, the same goes for vaccination. It is suitable for parents to be hesitant for their children and research about vaccination.

However, parents also need to understand that they need to have open opinions about all information found. They cannot have a perspective on vaccination because there is always going to be misleading or even real information that supports their opinion.

For example, parents may find a website that give out facts like, without vaccines, children are at risk of death from measles and whooping cough. However, they may also find a website that gives out facts like if children are vaccinated, they can still get measles, which is also possible. It makes parents think about whether they should vaccinate their child, which leads them to pick their perspective.

To help parents decide on vaccination, Ontario has a mandatory class required for parents to deny vaccination, which is an excellent way to help parents understand the meaning of vaccination. However, after some time, parents found it insulting to have the government force them to be 're-educated,' which caused thousands of parents to deny vaccination after learning about it.

There is another way to help parents de, which is working with confirmation bias instead of against it. A study done by Zachary Horne and others at the University of Illinois showed parents pictures of children suffering from mumps, measles or rubella, and a parent's description of a child's illness to stop them from not vaccinating, which worked better than sharing information debunking the idea that vaccines are not safe.
